Wheat Agronomy Guidelines

Soil preparation:
Wheat performs best in well-drained loam to clay loam soils with moderate organic matter and a soil pH near 6.0 to 7.5. Fields should be ploughed and harrowed to create a fine, firm seedbed. Avoid waterlogging by maintaining surface drainage and leveling the field before sowing.

Irrigation:
Wheat requires timely irrigation during crown root initiation, tillering, booting, flowering, and grain filling. Excess irrigation can reduce root oxygen and increase disease pressure. Moisture stress during flowering and grain development can sharply reduce final yield.

Pest management:
Monitor for aphids, termites, armyworms, and foliar diseases such as rusts and blight. Use certified seed, crop rotation, field sanitation, and threshold-based pesticide application. Avoid repeated use of one pesticide class to reduce resistance risk.

Fertilizer use:
Apply balanced nutrition based on soil testing. Nitrogen is usually split across basal application and later growth stages, while phosphorus and potassium are commonly applied at sowing. Zinc may be required in deficient soils. Overapplication of nitrogen can increase lodging and disease incidence.
